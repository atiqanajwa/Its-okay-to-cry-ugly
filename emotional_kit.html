🧘 regulation techniques

gentle ways to come back to yourself.

Not to suppress the feeling — but to ride it with grace.

🌬

breathing

4-7-8 Breathing

Activates your parasympathetic nervous system, slowing your heart rate and calming the stress response in minutes.

  • Inhale slowly through your nose for 4 counts
  • Hold your breath gently for 7 counts
  • Exhale completely through your mouth for 8 counts
  • Repeat 3–4 cycles, letting your shoulders drop
✦ good for: anxiety · overwhelm
📦

breathing

Box Breathing

Used by Navy SEALs and therapists alike — a simple square pattern that resets your nervous system fast.

  • Inhale for 4 counts
  • Hold for 4 counts
  • Exhale for 4 counts
  • Hold empty for 4 counts — then repeat
✦ good for: panic · stress
🌿

grounding

5-4-3-2-1 Grounding

Pulls you out of your head and back into your body by engaging all five senses in the present moment.

  • Name 5 things you can see
  • Name 4 things you can touch (and feel them)
  • Name 3 things you can hear
  • Name 2 things you can smell
  • Name 1 thing you can taste
✦ good for: dissociation · overwhelm
🖊

mind-based

Name It to Tame It

Research by neuroscientist Dr. Dan Siegel shows that labeling your emotion reduces its intensity by activating your prefrontal cortex.

  • Pause and ask: what exactly is this feeling?
  • Get specific — not "bad," but "ashamed" or "lonely"
  • Say it aloud or write it down
  • Notice the subtle softening that follows
✦ good for: all emotions
🤲

body-based

Self-Soothing Touch

Physical touch releases oxytocin and signals safety to your nervous system — even your own touch counts.

  • Place one hand over your heart
  • Feel the warmth and gentle pressure
  • Try saying softly: "I'm here. You're safe."
  • Stay here for 30–60 seconds
✦ good for: grief · loneliness · fear
🧊

body-based

Cold Water Reset

Splashing cold water on your face or wrists activates the mammalian dive reflex, slowing your heart rate within seconds.

  • Go to a sink and run cold water
  • Splash it on your face or hold your wrists under
  • Take a slow breath as you feel the temperature shift
  • Repeat until you feel your body settle
✦ good for: panic · intense anger
🌊

mind-based

Emotion Surfing

From Dialectical Behaviour Therapy (DBT) — emotions are waves, not permanent states. You don't have to stop them, just ride them.

  • Notice the emotion without judging it
  • Observe where it lives in your body
  • Watch it rise — and know it will fall
  • Let it move through you like a wave
✦ good for: intense emotions · sadness
🦶

grounding

Feet on the Floor

One of the simplest, most effective grounding techniques for highly sensitive people who get caught in emotional spirals.

  • Sit or stand with both feet flat on the ground
  • Press down gently — feel the floor beneath you
  • Notice the temperature, texture, solidity
  • Remind yourself: "I am here. I am safe. This will pass."
✦ good for: spiraling · dissociation
😮‍💨

breathing

Physiological Sigh

Dr. Andrew Huberman's research shows this double-inhale breath is the fastest way to reduce physiological stress in real time.

  • Take a full inhale through your nose
  • At the top, sniff in a small extra inhale
  • Now exhale slowly and completely through your mouth
  • Feel your body drop as you breathe out
✦ good for: instant calm · stress spikes

❓ frequently asked questions

am I really too sensitive?

Questions we hear often — answered with the honesty and gentleness you deserve.

Am I too sensitive for my own good?
No — but you may be using your sensitivity without enough support. Being highly sensitive means your nervous system processes the world more deeply than average. That's a neurological trait, not a character flaw. The challenge isn't your sensitivity; it's living in a world that wasn't designed with HSPs in mind. Your feelings are valid. Your intensity is real. And you are not too much.
Why do I cry so easily compared to others?
HSPs have more active mirror neurons and stronger emotional reactivity in the brain. Crying isn't weakness — it's your body processing emotional information that others might simply not register at the same depth. You feel what others skim over. That's not a problem to fix. That's a depth of feeling most people never experience.
Is being highly sensitive the same as having anxiety?
Not exactly. High sensitivity is a neutral trait — it's how your nervous system is wired. Anxiety is a response that can develop when an HSP doesn't have adequate coping tools or a supportive environment. Many HSPs do experience anxiety, but sensitivity itself is not a disorder. It exists across cultures, genders, and species — it's been found in over 100 animal species.
Can I become less sensitive over time?
Sensitivity is largely innate — it's part of your nervous system's wiring. However, you can absolutely build resilience — the ability to feel deeply without being destabilised. Therapy, emotional literacy, self-care practices, and environments that honor your needs can transform sensitivity from a vulnerability into a genuine superpower.
Why do I need so much alone time to recover?
Because your nervous system takes in more data than most. Social interactions, sensory input, emotional currents — you process all of it deeply. Alone time isn't antisocial; it's essential maintenance for your system. Think of it like charging a phone that uses more battery than average. You need more charge time, not a new phone.
How do I explain my sensitivity to people who don't understand?
Try this: "I process things more deeply than most people — emotions, sounds, social situations. It means I feel things strongly and sometimes need more recovery time. It's not personal and it's not something I can easily turn off." You don't owe anyone a full explanation. But naming it calmly helps others meet you where you are.
