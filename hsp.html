HSP explained

what is a
highly sensitive person?

If you've ever been told you're "too sensitive," cried at a commercial, or needed hours to decompress after a busy day — this page is for you. High sensitivity is not a flaw. It is a deeply human trait.

science-backed 8 min read Dr. Elaine Aron DOES framework

01 — the origin

Where the idea came from

In 1996, psychologist Dr. Elaine Aron gave a name to something millions of people had always felt but never had words for. She called it Sensory Processing Sensitivity — and the people who carry it, Highly Sensitive Persons (HSPs). About 1 in 5 people are HSPs. It's not a flaw. It's not a disorder. It's simply the way some nervous systems are wired.

It's also not new. Researchers have found this trait in over 100 animal species. It's an evolutionary advantage — every group benefits from having members who notice more, think before acting, and pick up on things others miss.

🔬

Dr. Elaine N. Aron

Research Psychologist  ·  Author of The Highly Sensitive Person (1996)

"The highly sensitive person has a nervous system that notices more — but it is not dysfunctional. It is differently tuned."

02 — the DOES model

The four pillars of high sensitivity

Dr. Aron identified four things that define an HSP. She named the model DOES. If all four feel familiar, you're probably one of us.

Depth of Processing

You slow down and think deeply. A quick chat can stay with you for days. You notice connections and meanings that go right over most people's heads.

Overstimulation

The more you take in, the faster you burn out. Crowded places or a packed schedule can wipe you out — and you need real quiet time to recover.

Emotional Reactivity & Empathy

You feel things strongly. Other people's emotions get into you — their stress makes you tense, their happiness lifts you. You care, and it shows.

Sensitivity to Subtleties

Small things stand out to you. A shift in someone's tone, the mood of a room, a detail no one else noticed — you pick it up before anyone else does.

03 — common traits

Signs you might be an HSP

Beyond DOES, here are six experiences HSPs recognise almost immediately. How many feel true for you?

🎵

Moved by music & art

A song, a film scene, or a sunset can bring you to tears. You feel beauty in a way that almost seems physical.

🪞

You read the room

You walk in somewhere and instantly know how people are feeling — even if nobody has said a word yet.

🔊

Sensory overload

Loud places, strong smells, or bright lights don't just bother you — they actually drain your energy.

⏳

You need alone time

After a busy or social day, you need quiet to recover. Not because you dislike people — your brain just processed a lot.

💭

You replay things

You go back over conversations and decisions long after they're done — not out of anxiety, but because your mind goes deep.

💬

Deep conversations only

Small talk feels hollow. You want real conversations. Surface-level interaction leaves you feeling oddly empty.

04 — myths & facts

Things people get wrong about HSPs

High sensitivity is widely misunderstood — sometimes even by HSPs themselves. Here are the most common myths, and what's actually true.

Being sensitive isn't a choice — it's wired into your nervous system. You can't think your way out of it, and you don't need to. Being strong and being sensitive are not opposites. They coexist all the time.

✅ Fact: HSPs tend to score high on care, attention to detail, and awareness — traits that make them excellent in roles that require empathy and precision.

High sensitivity isn't a condition or an illness — it doesn't appear in any medical handbook. It's a personality trait, like being creative or detail-oriented. It can sometimes overlap with anxiety, but they are not the same thing.

✅ Fact: Dr. Aron calls SPS a neutral trait — not a problem. In the right environment, it's genuinely a strength.

Introversion is about social energy. High sensitivity is about how deeply you process the world. They overlap — but they're different things. Plenty of HSPs are extroverts who love people but still need quiet time after too much input.

✅ Fact: Many HSPs are performers, teachers, and public figures who thrive socially — and still need time to decompress afterward.

Men are just as likely to be HSPs. The difference is that boys and men are more often told to hide their sensitivity — so it goes unrecognised, and they go unsupported.

✅ Fact: Research consistently shows that high sensitivity is equally common across all genders.

HSPs who grow up in a supportive environment often do better than average — they're more careful, more empathetic, and more tuned in to the people around them. Context matters more than most people realise.

✅ Fact: Scientists call this "differential susceptibility" — sensitive people are more affected by their environment, for better and for worse.

05 — living well

Caring for your sensitive self

High sensitivity isn't something to fix. It's something to understand and work with. These four things make a real difference.

🌿

Protect your downtime

You need more rest than most people. That's not weakness — it's just how your nervous system works. Build real breaks into your day, every day.

🔕

Lower the noise

Turn off notifications. Skip the heavy news cycle. Keep your space calm. Small changes like these add up when you feel everything more deeply.

✍️

Write it out

When feelings are too big to say out loud, writing helps. A journal slows down the mental spiral and helps you understand what you're actually going through.

🌙

Sleep first, always

Everything feels harder when you're tired — and for HSPs, that's doubled. If you can protect one thing in your routine, make it sleep.
